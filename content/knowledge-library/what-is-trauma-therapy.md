# What is trauma therapy?

**Category:** Trauma · **Read time:** 6 min

*Trauma therapy helps people understand what happened, how they adapted, and what may now need safety, support and integration.*

---

When people hear the phrase "trauma therapy", many imagine being asked to relive the worst moments of their lives in detail, week after week, until something finally shifts. It is one of the most common fears people bring to a first conversation with me, and it is worth addressing immediately: that is not what trauma therapy is.

Trauma therapy is not about forcing someone to relive painful experiences. It begins by creating enough steadiness for the body and mind to notice what has been carried. Before we go anywhere near what happened, we build the conditions in which it becomes possible to look at it without being overwhelmed by it.

## Trauma is not only the event.

One of the most useful ideas in trauma work is this: trauma is not simply what happened to you. It is what happened inside you as a result — and what you had to do to survive it.

Two people can live through the same experience and carry it very differently. What shapes the impact is not only the event itself but the age we were, the support we had, whether we could act or had to freeze, and whether anyone helped us make sense of it afterwards. An experience becomes traumatic when it overwhelms our capacity to process it — when it is too much, too fast, or too alone.

This is why I am cautious about ranking experiences. A person who grew up in an unpredictable home may carry as much in their nervous system as someone who survived a single catastrophic event. The question in therapy is never "was it bad enough to count?" The question is "what did you have to do to get through, and what is that costing you now?"

## People arrive in different ways.

Some people arrive with clear memories. They know what happened and can name it, even if talking about it feels impossible.

Others arrive with anxiety, shutdown, anger, shame, exhaustion or a sense of being far away from themselves — without any obvious story attached. They may say things like "nothing that bad ever happened to me" while describing a life organised entirely around staying safe, staying useful or staying invisible.

Both starting points are valid. Trauma does not always announce itself as memory. Sometimes it shows up as a pattern: relationships that repeat, a body that will not settle, a success that never feels safe to enjoy. Trauma therapy can begin from a story or from a symptom. The work finds its shape either way.

## Trauma therapy begins with safety.

Safety is not a warm-up phase we rush through to get to the "real work". Safety is the real work — at least at first.

When something overwhelming happens, the nervous system learns that the world is dangerous, and it keeps applying that lesson long after the danger has passed. A trauma-informed approach respects this. We do not ask the body to open a door before it trusts that it can close it again.

In practice, this means the early stages of therapy are about steadiness: understanding what brings you in, learning what helps you feel grounded, noticing what happens in your body when difficult material comes near, and building a relationship in which you feel genuinely accompanied rather than examined. For some people this phase is brief. For others it is the majority of the work, and that is not a failure — it is often the deepest repair.

## The work is paced around the nervous system.

A trauma-informed approach respects protection. We do not rush past the parts of a person that learned to survive.

If you dissociate, shut down, or become flooded when certain material arises, that is not resistance or weakness. It is your nervous system doing exactly what it learned to do. Good trauma therapy works with those responses rather than against them — slowing down, titrating how much we touch at a time, and returning to steadiness before going further.

The aim is to stay within what is sometimes called the window of tolerance: enough activation that something real is being worked with, not so much that you are simply re-experiencing the original overwhelm. Reliving trauma without support is not processing. It is repetition.

## Adaptations were once solutions.

Much of trauma therapy is not about the past at all. It is about the present-day patterns that grew out of it.

Hypervigilance was once a way of predicting danger. People-pleasing was once a way of staying connected to someone unpredictable. Numbness was once the only available anaesthetic. Perfectionism, control, self-sufficiency, avoidance — these are rarely character flaws. They are usually old solutions that outlived the problem they solved.

When we understand a pattern as protection, something softens. Self-blame quietens. And a pattern that is understood becomes something we can gradually loosen, rather than something we have to fight.

## What the work can include.

There is no single method called "trauma therapy". It is an umbrella over several ways of working, and in my practice these are woven together rather than applied one at a time.

Relational work is the foundation: the steady, repeated experience of being met without judgement, which is itself corrective for anyone whose wounds happened in relationship. Alongside this I draw on Transactional Analysis, which gives us shared language for the patterns and early decisions that grew around what happened. Body-based understanding matters too, because trauma lives in the nervous system as much as in the story — so we pay attention to breath, sensation, grounding and state, not only to words. And where specific memories remain active and intrusive, structured processing such as EMDR can help the nervous system file them as past rather than present.

Which of these leads at any given moment depends on you — your history, your pace, and what your system can use. The method is never more important than the relationship.

## What change looks like.

Trauma therapy helps separate what belongs to the past from what is happening now, so life has more room to move.

Change is rarely a single dramatic breakthrough. More often it is quieter: sleeping a little better, snapping less quickly, noticing a trigger and having a choice about it, letting someone closer than felt possible before. Memories that once arrived with full force begin to feel like something that happened, rather than something that is still happening.

Integration is a good word for the destination. Not forgetting, not erasing, but being able to hold your history as part of your story instead of the whole of it.

## How long does it take?

Honestly: it varies, and anyone who promises a fixed timetable is selling something. A single overwhelming event in an otherwise supported life may resolve in focused, relatively brief work. Trauma that began early and ran for years — the kind that shaped how you attach, trust and feel — usually needs a longer relationship, because what was learned over years is relearned over time, not over weekends.

What I can promise is that we review the work together, openly, and that you will never be strung along. The pace belongs to you.

## You do not need to be ready.

People often wait to begin therapy until they feel ready to "deal with it all". You do not need that. You need only enough curiosity to have a first conversation, and a therapist who will pace the work around you rather than around a protocol.

If parts of this article describe your life, that recognition is itself a beginning. Understanding what happened — and how you adapted — is often the first step towards something changing.
