# Transactional Analysis and physis.

**Category:** Transactional Analysis · **Read time:** 6 min

*Transactional Analysis offers language for patterns, relationship and the natural movement towards growth.*

---

Transactional Analysis — TA for short — is the model at the heart of my training and my practice. It was developed by the psychiatrist Eric Berne in the 1950s and 60s, and it rests on a conviction I share completely: people make sense, and people can understand themselves.

Berne wanted psychology out of the locked cabinet. He believed a person in therapy deserved to understand what was being understood about them — that the map should be shared, not kept behind the desk. TA's language is therefore deliberately plain: Parent, Adult, Child; scripts, strokes, games. Simple words for deep structures — chosen so that the person doing the changing can hold the map in their own hands.

## TA helps patterns become visible.

Transactional Analysis is a psychotherapy model that pays attention to relationship, communication, life patterns and the meanings people learned early. It can offer language for things a person has felt for years but never quite had words for.

A few of its central ideas, briefly.

TA suggests we move between three ego states. The Parent holds what we absorbed from the big people of our childhood — their voices of care and their voices of criticism, often replaying word for word. The Child holds how we felt and adapted back then — the spontaneity and the survival strategies alike. The Adult is our capacity to respond to this moment as it actually is. Much distress involves the past states running the present: a boss's raised eyebrow answered by a five-year-old's dread, a partner's mistake answered by a parent's contempt.

TA also speaks of the life script — the story a young child writes, from limited evidence, about who they are and what they can expect. *I must be strong. I mustn't need. I'll never really belong.* Script decisions were brilliant solutions to a child's real predicament. The trouble is that they keep running the show decades after the predicament has ended. In this sense TA and trauma-informed thinking agree completely: patterns were once protection.

And TA notices games — the repeating, painful loops two people can run together without either choosing them, each round confirming the old familiar ending: *see, I always end up rejected; see, no one can be trusted.* Naming the game is often the first moment a person realises the ending is not fate.

## Physis points towards growth.

And then there is physis — my favourite idea in the whole model, and the one that gives this article its title.

Physis is an old Greek word for nature, growth, the force that makes things become what they are. Berne borrowed it to name something he could not otherwise explain: the drive in people towards health, growth and healing that persists even in the most difficult lives. It is the same movement you see in a plant growing towards light through a crack in concrete — not because the concrete is kind, but because growing is what it does.

I have seen physis in every person I have ever worked with, without exception. It is there in the very act of searching for a therapist, in the discomfort that says *this is no longer enough*, in the stubborn, sometimes exhausted refusal to fully give up on life going differently. Even symptoms often contain it: the breakdown that was, in truth, a bid for a different life.

In TA, physis describes the natural movement towards healing and growth. Therapy does not create that movement. It creates the conditions in which it can begin again.

That sentence carries my whole philosophy of the work. I am not the source of your growth, and no therapist is. The drive is already in you — it has simply been blocked, discouraged, or pressed into the service of mere survival. The work of therapy is to find where the growth got blocked, understand what the block once protected, and let the movement resume.

## Strokes, and why we settle for painful ones.

One more TA idea earns its place here, because it quietly explains so much: the stroke. A stroke is any unit of recognition — a greeting, a touch, praise, criticism, even a glare. Berne's insight was that human beings need strokes the way we need food; recognition is not a luxury but a biological hunger.

And here is the uncomfortable arithmetic that follows: to a hungry person, a negative stroke is better than none. A child who cannot win warmth will learn to reliably provoke anger, because being scolded is at least being seen — invisibility is worse. Carry that forward thirty years and you find adults who unconsciously farm criticism, conflict or crisis, not because they enjoy pain, but because it is the recognition system they learned to operate.

Seen this way, some of the most puzzling patterns — picking fights when things are calm, sabotaging success just as it lands — stop looking like self-destruction and start looking like what they are: an old, once-sensible strategy for staying fed.

## How TA works in the room.

In practice, TA therapy is relational and collaborative. We use the ideas together, out loud — nothing is diagnosed at you from behind a screen of jargon. We contract clearly for what you want to change, because in TA the client's own goal, not the therapist's theory, steers the work.

And because script was learned in relationship, it changes in relationship. The therapy relationship itself becomes the place where the old pattern shows up — the deference, the testing, the bracing for criticism — and where, this time, something different happens. New experience, repeated, is what rewrites old conclusions. Insight draws the map; the relationship walks the territory.

A brief example of how the ideas combine. Imagine someone who learned early that love arrived only when they were useful. Script decision: *I matter when I'm needed.* Ego states: a Parent voice that calls rest laziness, a Child who feels a flicker of panic whenever they are not helping. The game: volunteering for everything, burning out, feeling resentful and unappreciated — final proof that giving is never repaid, and round it goes. In therapy we would name this pattern together, in plain language; trace it to the predicament it once solved; and then use the relationship itself — a place where they are valued without performing usefulness — to let a different conclusion form. That is TA in motion: map, meaning, and new experience.

## Why this model.

I work integratively — TA alongside trauma-informed practice, EMDR, attachment thinking and the neuroscience of the body — but TA remains the spine, because of what it assumes about people. It assumes you were born whole. It assumes your difficulties are adaptations, not defects. It assumes you can understand your own mind. And it assumes that, given the right conditions, something in you already knows the way and has been trying to take it all along.

Berne summarised the goal of TA in a word that has never been bettered: autonomy — the recovered capacity for awareness, spontaneity and intimacy. Not a personality transplant, but the person you were built to be, back at the controls.

Physis, in other words. The path is different for everyone — but the movement is in all of us, and it is patient. It waits for conditions, not permission.
