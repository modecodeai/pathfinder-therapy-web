# What happens in a first therapy session?

**Category:** Therapy Explained · **Read time:** 5 min

*A first session is a steady conversation about what has brought you here, what you need, and whether the work feels right.*

---

Most people are nervous before a first session. That is worth saying plainly, because many arrive assuming their nervousness is a bad sign — evidence that they are not ready, or that therapy is not for them. It is neither. Walking into a room to talk about your inner life with a stranger is an unusual thing to do. Some apprehension is simply accurate.

So here is what actually happens, at least in my practice, so the unknown can be a little less unknown.

## The first session is not a test.

You do not need to arrive with a polished story. The first session is a place to begin gently.

There is no examination to pass, no required level of insight, and no correct way to describe your life. Some people talk fluently for fifty minutes; others find that the words come slowly, or not at all at first. Both are fine. Silence in a therapy room is not awkwardness to be filled — it is often where something is being felt before it can be said.

We may talk about what has been difficult, what you hope could change, and what would help the process feel safe enough. I will ask some questions, but you remain in charge of how much you share. You do not need to tell your whole story in the first session — or the tenth. Depth is earned by the relationship over time, not demanded at the door.

## What I am paying attention to.

Before the session itself, the practical worries deserve an answer too. You do not need to prepare notes, rehearse an opening line, or decide in advance what the "main issue" is. If you dry up, I will help. If you cry, nothing has gone wrong. If you laugh at something dark, that is allowed too — humour is often how survivors carry things. The session is fifty minutes, and it belongs to you.

While we talk, part of my attention is on the content — the events, the relationships, the feelings you describe. But another part is on something quieter: how it is for you to be here at all.

Do you rush past painful things or circle them carefully? Does your body tense when a particular subject comes near? Do you apologise for having needs? None of this is judged. All of it is information — early glimpses of the patterns that shaped you, appearing naturally in the room. People make sense, and the first session is where I begin learning how you do.

I will usually also ask about practical things: what support you have around you, anything about your health that matters, what you have tried before and how it went. If therapy did not help you in the past, that is especially useful to talk about, so we can do something different this time.

## It is also a chance to notice fit.

Therapy depends on relationship. The first meeting gives both people a chance to sense whether the pace, tone and approach feel useful.

Decades of research on what makes therapy work point again and again to the same factor: the quality of the relationship between therapist and client matters more than any specific technique. Which means the question you should be quietly asking in a first session is not "is this therapist impressive?" but "do I feel a little steadier, a little more understood, in this room?"

You are allowed to notice that the fit is not right. A good therapist will not be wounded by that; finding the right person is part of the work, not a detour from it.

## Practicalities, briefly.

Towards the end of a first session we usually talk about the frame: how often we would meet, whether we work online or in person, fees, confidentiality and what happens between sessions. Clear agreements are not bureaucracy. They are part of what makes the space feel safe enough to use.

There is no pressure to decide everything immediately. You can take time after the session to notice how it settled in you. Sometimes the most honest information arrives on the walk home.

One question worth asking me — and any therapist you meet — is how they work and why. You are entitled to a plain answer. In my case: I work relationally and integratively, drawing on Transactional Analysis, trauma-informed practice, EMDR and an understanding of the body, and I believe the pace of the work belongs to the person in the other chair. A therapist who cannot explain their approach without jargon is telling you something useful too.

## After the session.

People are sometimes surprised by what a first session stirs. You may leave feeling lighter — relief at having finally said things aloud is real and common. You may also feel tired, tender or unexpectedly emotional later that day; speaking about your life, even gently, moves things that have been still for a long time. Both responses are normal, and neither predicts how the therapy will go.

It helps not to book anything demanding immediately afterwards if you can avoid it. A walk, a quiet hour, a little space for the session to settle — these are not indulgences. They are part of letting the work land.

## What to bring.

Nothing, really — except perhaps one sentence: what is happening in your life that made you reach out now? "Now" is often the most revealing word. Something shifted, ended, returned or became unsustainable. That is where we begin.

And if reaching out is the part you keep postponing — writing the enquiry, deleting it, writing it again — know that this hesitation is itself ordinary, and it usually says something worth understanding. Send the imperfect message. The first session will take care of the rest.

Everything else — the history, the understanding, the change — grows from there, at a pace that respects how you are built.
